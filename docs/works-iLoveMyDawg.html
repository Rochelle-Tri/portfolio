Design Outside The Western Lens

At first glance, I Love My Dawg appears to be a website prototype for a fictional veterinary service. In reality, this prototype functions as a container for my research into the differences and similarities between Western (specifically North American) and German service-based website design. This project explores the concept of localization in design—how visual and functional design elements shift depending on the cultural context they’re created for.

Research Results

In summary of my research findings, I found that generally, North American service-based website design tends to prioritize user-friendliness, visual appeal, and emotional engagement through bold graphics and marketing-driven content. In contrast, Germans often emphasise functionality, minimalism, and precision, with a utilitarian approach that ensures legitimacy and social proof.

Picking A Research Topic

This project was developed during my field school in Berlin. After early-stage research and writing a proposal that outlined three potential directions, I chose to examine German vs. Western service-based website design. It was a perspective I hadn’t explored much in my coursework, and I wanted to challenge my assumptions about design across different modern societies.

Designing The Container

Initially, I planned to present my research as a written paper, but after feedback from my professor encouraging me to explore expressive mediums, I pivoted to a speculative website prototype. I sketched and wireframed potential layouts and conducted a comparative analysis of veterinary websites from both Germany and North America. This helped me understand what kind of content these services typically present, and how cultural expectations influence visual style, tone, and user flow.

Stylizing the Prototype

I selected a blue and brown color palette—blue to convey reliability, trust, and health, and brown as a warm, complementary tone that subtly evokes animals (paw prints, fur, and earthiness). This helped ground the visual language in the veterinary theme while reflecting culturally appropriate design cues.

Personalizing My Research

One of the biggest challenges I faced was making my research feel unique beyond simply restating what I found in online articles and design blogs. To address this, I expanded my methodology beyond secondary sources and conducted interviews with German locals and my Canadian classmates about their personal experiences using German vs. Western digital platforms. Most of my interviewees were students and tech-savvy users, offering useful insights into usability expectations and visual norms in each region.

Taking Creative Liberty

If you take a closer look at the imagery in the prototype, you’ll notice that many of the animals are internet memes rather than stock-photos. I deliberately chose these humorous photos to inject personality into the project. Unlike my usual coursework, this wasn’t a functional deliverable for a real-world client—so I used the opportunity to be playful, personal, and most importantly, make my presentation memorable.

Reflection

As my first independent research-based project, I learned how to gather and interpret information through multiple channels, including interviews, content audits, and observational studies. In hindsight, I wish I had conducted more interviews to broaden my data pool. Doing this kind of research in a foreign country felt intimidating at first, but it also gave me the motivation to grow in that area moving forward.

Most importantly, this project taught me about localization and its deep ties to culture—not just in design, but in how people expect to interact with information. Studying Germany’s design history and visual language in context helped me recognize my own design biases and showed me how powerful it is to adapt design for diverse user groups.
