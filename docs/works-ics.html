Introduction to Incident Command System 100

Team

Learning and Development

Timeline

Tools

Figma, Articulate 360, MS Word

Key Responsibilities

My role was to design and develop interactive eLearning content for the City of Surrey’s internal Learning Management System (LMS). I was tasked with creating two interactive training modules for the Surrey Fire Department: “Introduction to Incident Command System (ICS) 100” and “Emergency Operations Centre (EOC) Overview.” These modules were developed to introduce foundational emergency management skills to exempt staff and corporate managers—many of whom had limited experience with emergency response protocols. With emergency management becoming increasingly relevant across sectors like healthcare and government, the goal was to provide accessible, foundational training to a broader professional audience.

Translating and Visualizing the Modules

At the start of the project, I was provided with detailed storyboards containing the course content and copy. My responsibility was to interpret and translate this static information into engaging, interactive eLearning modules using Articulate Storyline 360. To begin, I sketched layout drafts and annotated questions to clarify requirements or suggest content enhancements. Early in the project, I used Figma to prototype screens more visually before transferring them into Storyline. As I became more comfortable with the software, I began designing directly in Storyline, using Figma only when creating custom visual assets that were too time-consuming to build natively.

Working Collaboratively

I managed my own production schedule and set internal deadlines to stay on track. Throughout the process, I held regular online check-ins with my client to present progress, ask questions, and gather feedback. Additionally, I shared drafts using Review 360, allowing both my client and teammates to interact with the modules and provide user feedback through multiple iterations.

Solving for Usability and Learnability

One of the more technically challenging aspects of the modules was designing the end-of-course quizzes. The Storyline update at this time offered limited customization options within quiz scenes, such as restricting font size in dropdown components. To address this, I consulted with my client and proposed simplifying and shortening the quiz answer text. After review, she revised the original quiz content to ensure consistency and legibility within the design constraints, without altering the meaning or intent of the questions.

Getting the Knowledge To Stick

The ICS 100 module presented another design challenge due to its longer length compared to the EOC Overview. Recognizing that users might take breaks or forget key information before reaching the final quiz, I proposed adding 1–2 mini quiz questions at the end of each chapter. This decision helped reinforce learning and improved knowledge retention for users unfamiliar with emergency protocols. My client supported this idea, and we implemented it across all chapters.

Reflection

This was my first time leading a client-facing project, and it taught me how to balance independent design work with ongoing stakeholder communication, iteration, and usability testing. I gained hands-on experience with Articulate 360, and I especially enjoyed discovering custom solutions and building unique interactions beyond what I was initially taught. A standout moment came when my supervisor complimented one of my timeline solutions and mentioned it would be considered for future training modules.

If given more time, I would have conducted additional user testing to further refine the module’s usability and learning outcomes. This project sparked a deeper interest in the intersection of design, education, and accessibility. It showed me how UX design can support meaningful learning experiences, and how education itself is a powerful tool in shaping more informed, prepared communities.
