01 Overview

My main tasks were to ideate, wireframe, and design mockups for the guidelines and patterns pages within the component library, aka a Storybook, an open source tool for building UI components and pages in isolation. These pages reflected Nokia’s visual branding and philosophy while also providing universal standards on design subjects such as empty states, localization, and iconography. The goal of this library was to make an online source available for designers and developers to easily understand and learn what Nokia’s design standards and components are, instead of reading through their long text-heavy manual.

02 Process

Using the info from the procedures and rules that were already established in Nokia, I conducted and gathered more informational research on the topics I had to format for the pages. This research included info explaining what the topic is, things to avoid and do when using components related to the topic, and general best practices. Using my research, I sketched out potential layout ideas that displayed the topics I found most important and relevant to Nokia.

I designed the mockups for my pages on Sketch and would upload them to Abstract for my peers to review and provide feedback. It would be a continuous loop of reviewing feedback and making changes to either the design or content on the page until it would be ready for me to send the final artboard to the developers through our work management tool, Jira.

03 Challenges + Decisions

There was a steep learning curve at the beginning of my co-op term since I was not used to navigating through the digital design platforms and understood fairly little of the design related procedures and vocabulary that my team used. To get caught up on what I needed to know, I set aside a lot of time reading through Nokia’s own design manuals and guidelines. I also did extra research on other design systems such as Adobe Spectrum and Shopify’s Polaris React.

The localization page was one of the most frustrating pages to complete because there was very little written on the topic from Nokia’s existing design best practices manual. I got the content I needed by asking questions from different designers and developers from other teams who were more knowledgeable on certain topics I needed insight on when I was not able to find any useful or relevant info from other sources.

There were times when I would not understand what my colleagues would be telling me when giving feedback or instructions. In those cases, we would call and go over things together, which allowed me to feel more comfortable asking questions and explaining my ideas by visually presenting them on a screen.

04 Reflection

Overall, my first co-op taught me that being able to adapt to new situations and willing to put in the effort to learn and improve is more important than anything. I learned how to get used to the work environment and etiquette quickly so that I would be able to keep up with my colleagues while also learning about the new material. In terms of actual design, I learned a lot of key terms and components and how to integrate them into my future projects such as cards, empty states and notifications.

There were some occasions where I felt the need to get as many art boards done as quickly as possible before the deadline which caused me to work more hastily and make minor mistakes in my design process. If I were to work on this project again, I would take more time reviewing my work carefully before putting up my drafts on Abstract and pinging my peers to provide feedback, so that I could reduce the amount of times I had to ping them.
