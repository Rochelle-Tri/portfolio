01 Overview

My team and I were tasked with a simulated job to help the design agency IDEO form a new interactive solution for their client Science World, a local museum that focuses on fun exhibits that make learning science-related topics easier for a young audience. To deliver a thorough and detailed design pitch, my team and I were to do in depth research on the institution and its clients, identify what problems could arise/that they already have, and come up with solutions through a series of brainstorming and other ideation methods.

The primary roles and tasks that I performed for my team included: conducting the research, creating the journey maps and user personas, copywriting for the weekly design pitch decks, and group management.

02 Process

To understand the problems our clients were experiencing, our team conducted field research online and at their institution. I interviewed people ranging from a variety of ages and demographics about their personal experiences at Science World and what they would like from them to improve on. Our initial framing based on our grouping exercises was that Science Worldâ€™s target audience were families with young children, which leaves people out of that age range feeling excluded or bored from the exhibits. I mainly worked on the user personas using the data based off of our final groupings and framings.

After reflecting on our past framings, we shifted our attention on how we could improve the user experience of adults/caregivers and their children instead of those outside of this group since they were not the primary audience. We decided to conduct another round of field research, this time narrowing it down to only children and parents. I interviewed parents and those in elementary school who had visited the institution in the past. Instead of focusing on what the parents wanted differently from Science World, I paid more attention to their own personal feelings and experiences throughout the institution.

Our new insights were that parents/educators often have a difficult time enjoying themselves at the exhibits because they are either too busy managing their children or are worried about how much they are actually learning. The concepts for our three products were a virtual map, a headband that tracks learning, and an interface that physically projects the info from an exhibit to the participant. After we came up with our three ideas, I was in charge of creating the journey maps, while also collaborating on the storyboards.

My team decided to explore the idea of an interface that allows a combined engaging and learning experience for both the child and their caretaker. However, we still wanted a higher emphasis on the child as they were the primary audience of Science World and were the caretakersâ€™ main priority in ensuring that they have a good experience there. The solution we came up with for our problem statement (a lack of exhibits for families to interact together) was a mobile app that gives access to more optimal learning for all ages in families in order for both ages to enjoy the exhibits. On top of the education sector, we thought that having a feature on transportation would benefit those who are coming to the institution for the first time and want to maximize their time by knowing how to quickly navigate to the exhibits that interest them.

Since I had the best understanding of the imposed problem statements and solutions during the final week, I focused on doing the copywriting in the design pitch deck after my team and I reflected on improving our design solution. I also refined the journey maps, collaborated on the wireframes, and brought together the final copy of the user flowchart after the drafts from each member were made.

03 Challenges + Decisions

A major challenge was how to collaborate as a team remotely since we were not able to have efficient meetings in the beginning. We had extremely varying work schedules and also lived in different time zones. To make our availability more transparent to each other, I had my group and I fill out an online calendar to figure out which times and days we should have our meetings. As the project progressed, I got a better sense of my group matesâ€™ skills and was also able to assign them different roles so that we could achieve our goals more efficiently. Since a member of our team had to leave the course very suddenly, I also took it to myself to fill in their role as the main content writer and project manager.

The most significant changes we made were the user personas and the concepts of our user interface. In the beginning, our personas did not accurately portray our audience or their frustrations and we did not do a good job showcasing them in our design pitch. After seeking feedback and conducting more research, I understood that while explaining our personas, I had to provide evidence from our data to make them appear more credible and realistic.

04 Learning Outcome

Emphasizing with the audience that I was designing for was more difficult than I thought. I learnt that it required very deep reflection and analysis to understand what potential problems/frustrations they could have and that I should not make assumptions about them.

Since this was my first major user experience design project, I learned much on the topic and greatly developed my leadership and communication skills through the challenges I faced. Due to the heavy expectations on each design pitch every week, I was able to better develop my time management and problem solving skills using the given feedback from every critique. This project allowed me to learn how to quickly adapt to stressful situations and understand when to take direction in a project. In the end, I felt that I was able to successfully do my part and not only greatly improved the user personas and final pitch but also managed my team in the most accommodating way possible.
